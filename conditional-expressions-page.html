Conditional Expressions:
Unless, Provided That, As Long As

Introduction

Conditional expressions show that one action depends on another. In English, we use words like unless, provided that, and as long as to talk about conditions. These words help us explain rules, requirements, and restrictions in daily life, work, and travel.

Explanation

Unless = If not

Meaning: We use unless to introduce a condition that prevents something from happening. It is similar to if not.

Structure: Unless + present/simple past, main clause

Examples:

✅ Unless you study, you will fail the exam. (= If you don't study, you will fail.)

✅ I won't go unless she invites me. (= If she doesn't invite me, I won't go.)

✅ You can't enter unless you have a ticket. (= If you don't have a ticket, you can't enter.)

Provided That = Only if

Meaning: We use provided that to say that something will happen only if a condition is met. It is often used in formal or business situations.

Structure: Main clause + provided that + condition

Examples:

✅ You can leave early provided that you finish your work. (= You can leave only if you finish your work.)

✅ We will lend you the money provided that you pay it back next month. (= Only if you pay it back next month, we will lend you the money.)

✅ You can stay here provided that you don't make too much noise. (= Only if you don't make noise, you can stay here.)

As Long As = If / On the condition that

Meaning: As long as is used to say that something will happen if a condition is met. It is similar to provided that but more informal.

Structure: Main clause + as long as + condition

Examples:

✅ You can borrow my car as long as you drive carefully. (= You can borrow my car if you drive carefully.)

✅ I'll be happy as long as I have coffee in the morning. (= If I have coffee in the morning, I'll be happy.)

✅ You can stay out late as long as you call me. (= If you call me, you can stay out late.)

Common Errors & How to Avoid Them

❌ Unless you will call me, I won't come. → (Incorrect: Don't use will after unless.)

✅ Unless you call me, I won't come.

❌ Provided that he will arrive on time, we can start. → (Incorrect: No will after provided that.)

✅ Provided that he arrives on time, we can start.

❌ You can go as long as if you finish your work. → (Incorrect: Don't use if with as long as.)

✅ You can go as long as you finish your work.

Useful Tips

✔ Think in simple conditions – Before speaking or writing, ask yourself: Am I talking about a rule (provided that), a flexible condition (as long as), or a negative condition (unless)?

✔ Practice with real situations – Imagine workplace or travel scenarios where these expressions are useful. Example: I will book the hotel provided that the price is good.

✔ Look for them in real English – Pay attention when reading news, emails, or contracts. You'll see these phrases often in conditions and agreements.

1. Complete the sentence: "You can borrow my laptop ________ you return it tomorrow."

A. unless
B. as long as
C. provided that if
Incorrect. "Unless" means "if not," so this would mean "You can borrow my laptop if you don't return it tomorrow," which doesn't make sense.
Correct! "As long as" is perfect here to set a condition for borrowing the laptop.
Incorrect. We don't use "if" with "provided that" - it would be just "provided that."

2. Which sentence is INCORRECT?

A. Unless it rains, we'll have a picnic tomorrow.
B. You can stay here provided that you'll be quiet.
C. I'll help you as long as you listen to my instructions.
This is correct usage of "unless."
Correct answer! This is incorrect because we don't use "will" after "provided that." It should be "provided that you are quiet."
This is correct usage of "as long as."

3. "__________ he apologizes, I won't speak to him again." Which is the best option?

A. Unless
B. As long as
C. Provided that
Correct! "Unless" is perfect here, meaning "If he doesn't apologize, I won't speak to him again."
Incorrect. "As long as" would mean you won't speak to him while he continues to apologize, which doesn't make sense in this context.
Incorrect. "Provided that" would suggest that not speaking to him is conditional on him apologizing, which is the opposite of what we want to express.

Conversation Starters

Here are some questions to practice using these conditional expressions in natural conversation:

  1. Travel Plans

    "What would be your dream vacation destination, and what conditions would need to be met for you to go there?" (Use provided that, as long as, unless)

  2. Work/Study Rules

    "What are some conditions your boss/teacher sets for you? For example, can you work from home as long as...?"

  3. Setting Boundaries

    "When lending things to friends or family, what conditions do you usually set? Would you lend your car provided that...?"

  4. Negotiating

    "Let's practice negotiating a rental agreement. What conditions would you set as a landlord or expect as a tenant?"

  5. Future Plans

    "What are your plans for next year? Will those plans change unless certain things happen?"

With practice, these conditionals will become natural in your conversations! 🚀
