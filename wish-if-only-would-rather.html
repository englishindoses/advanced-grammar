Wish, If Only, and Would Rather: Expressing Regrets and Desires

Introduction

These structures allow English speakers to talk about situations they want to change or hypothetical scenarios they desire. Understanding how to use "wish," "if only," and "would rather" helps you express regrets about the past, things you want to change in the present, and hopes for the future in a natural way.

In professional settings, social situations, and academic contexts, these structures are frequently used to discuss preferences, share feelings about decisions, and negotiate politely with others.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Express regrets about past situations using "wish" and "if only."
  • Talk about things you want to change in the present using appropriate verb forms.
  • Use "would rather" to express preferences in different time frames.

Explanation

Using "wish" and "if only" for present situations: We use these structures to talk about things we want to be different now.

Structure: wish/if only + past simple (or "could" + base verb)

  1. I wish I spoke better English. (I don't speak English well now.)
  2. She wishes she had more time to exercise. (She doesn't have enough time now.)
  3. If only the weather were warmer today. (The weather is cold now.)
  4. I wish I could swim. (I can't swim now.)

Native speaker note: In informal situations, native speakers sometimes say: "I wish I was taller" instead of the grammatically correct "I wish I were taller."

Using "wish" and "if only" for past regrets: We use these structures to express regret about past situations that cannot be changed.

Structure: wish/if only + past perfect (had + past participle)

  1. I wish I had studied harder for the exam. (I didn't study hard enough.)
  2. If only I had arrived earlier, I would have met your parents. (I arrived late and missed them.)
  3. She wishes she had taken that job offer last year. (She didn't take the job.)

Native speaker note: You might hear native speakers say: "I wish I never went there" in casual conversation, when the grammatically correct form would be "I wish I had never gone there."

Using "would rather" for preferences: We use this structure to express preferences between two or more options.

Structure:

  • would rather + base verb (for present/future)
    Example: I would rather work from home. (This is my preference now.)
  • would rather + have + past participle (for past)
    Example: She would rather have gone to the beach. (This was her preference then.)
  1. I would rather work from home than commute to the office. (This is my preference.)
  2. She would rather have gone to the conference herself. (This was her preference in the past.)
  3. Would you rather meet on Tuesday or Wednesday? (Which day do you prefer?)

Native speaker note: In casual speech, native speakers often contract "would rather" to "I'd rather" or even just "rather" in quick speech.
Example: "I'd rather stay home" or "Rather stay home than go out tonight."

Visual Comparison of Wish, If Only, and Would Rather

Wish/If only Past Past perfect I wish I had taken that opportunity.
Wish/If only Future Would/Could I wish it would stop raining soon.
Would rather Present/Future Base form I would rather stay home tonight.
Would rather Past Have + past participle I would rather have gone by train.
Would rather (others) Present/Future Past simple I'd rather you didn't smoke here.
Would rather (others) Past Past perfect I'd rather you had told me earlier.

Common Errors

1: Using present simple after "wish" for present situations

I wish I have more time to study.

I wish I had more time to study. ✓

The past simple is used after "wish" to talk about present wishes, not the present simple.

2: Using past simple instead of past perfect for past regrets

She wishes she went to university.

She wishes she had gone to university. ✓

When expressing regret about the past, we need the past perfect tense after "wish".

3: Mixing up verb forms with "would rather" for others' actions

I would rather you don't come late tomorrow.

I would rather you didn't come late tomorrow. ✓

When expressing preferences about other people's actions, we use past simple for present/future situations.

Useful Tips

📝 TIP 1: Remember the time shift: present situations use past tense forms, and past situations use past perfect forms after "wish" and "if only."

📝 TIP 2: "If only" is more emphatic and emotional than "wish," but follows the same grammar rules.

📝 TIP 3: For "would rather," use the simple form for your own preferences, but past tense forms when telling others what you want them to do.
