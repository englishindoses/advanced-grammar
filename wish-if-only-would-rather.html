Wish, If Only, and Would Rather: Expressing Regrets and Desires

Introduction

This lesson explores three powerful structures in English that allow you to express regrets about the present and past, wishes for different situations, and preferences between options. These expressions are essential for conveying nuanced feelings and hypothetical thinking in both professional and personal conversations.

You will frequently use these structures when discussing career choices, personal decisions, and when giving advice or feedback to others.

After this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Express regrets about present and past situations using "wish" and "if only"
  • Talk about desires for things to be different using appropriate verb tenses
  • Express preferences between options using "would rather"
  • Use these structures correctly in conversations and writing

Using "Wish" for Present and Past Situations

Use: "Wish" expresses regret or desire for situations that are not real. It's used when we want something to be different from what it actually is (present) or was (past).

Structure:

  • Present situations: wish + subject + past simple/continuous
  • Past situations: wish + subject + past perfect
  • Future situations: wish + subject + would/could + verb
  1. I wish I spoke better French. It would help me communicate with our international clients.
  2. She wishes she had more free time to spend with her family.
  3. The students wish they could access the online library from home.

Using "If Only" for Stronger Regrets and Desires

Use: "If only" works the same way as "wish" but expresses stronger emotions, often indicating a sense of regret, longing, or desire for something that isn't possible or likely.

Structure:

  • Present situations: if only + subject + past simple/continuous
  • Past situations: if only + subject + past perfect
  • Future situations: if only + subject + would/could + verb
  1. If only I had prepared better for the presentation yesterday.
  2. If only we lived closer to the beach! We could go swimming more often.
  3. If only the professor would explain the concept one more time.

Using "Would Rather" for Preferences

Use: "Would rather" expresses preferences between options. It can be used to talk about your own preferences or what you want someone else to do.

Structure:

  • Personal preference (same subject): would rather + verb (base form)
  • Preference for others (different subject): would rather + subject + past simple
  1. I would rather work from home than commute to the office every day.
  2. She would rather her children studied more during the week.
  3. The teacher would rather students submitted their assignments on time.

Using "Would Rather Have" for Past Preferences

Use: "Would rather have" expresses preferences about past situations, often indicating regret about choices that were made.

Structure: would rather have + past participle

  1. I would rather have discussed the issue in private instead of during the meeting.
  2. They would rather have stayed at home than gone to the party last night.
  3. He would rather have taken the literature course than the history one last semester.

Common Errors

I wish I have more time.

I wish I had more time. ✓

After "wish" referring to the present, use past simple (had), not present tense (have).

If only she would studied harder last term.

If only she had studied harder last term. ✓

For past regrets, use past perfect (had studied) after "if only", not "would + past participle".

I would rather to go by bus.

I would rather go by bus. ✓

After "would rather", use the base form of the verb without "to".

She would rather that we go home now.

She would rather we went home now. ✓

When "would rather" refers to someone else's action, use past simple (went) without "that".

I wish I can speak Chinese.

I wish I could speak Chinese. ✓

Use "could" not "can" after "wish" to express ability.

Useful Tips

  1. Think about time reference - The tense you use after "wish" and "if only" depends on when the situation happens: past simple for present situations, past perfect for past situations.
  2. Notice the emotion - "If only" generally expresses stronger feelings than "wish" and often shows more regret or longing.
  3. Mind the structure after "would rather" - Remember to use the base form of the verb directly after "would rather" (no "to"), but use past simple when talking about what you want someone else to do.
  4. Watch your tenses - One of the most common mistakes is using the wrong tense after these expressions. Remember that we use past tenses even when talking about present situations.

📝 GRAMMAR TIP: To remember which tense to use after "wish," think: "One step back in time." Present situations use past tense, and past situations use past perfect.

Incorrect. The -ing form (giving) cannot be used directly after "wish".
Incorrect. Present perfect (has given) is not used after "wish".

A. eat
B. ate
C. eating
D. to eat
Incorrect. When "would rather" refers to a different subject (her children), we use past simple, not base form.
Correct! When "would rather" refers to what someone else should do, we use past simple (ate).
Incorrect. The -ing form is not used after "would rather" with a different subject.
Incorrect. "To + infinitive" is not used after "would rather" with any subject.

Incorrect. "Would know" is not a correct form after "if only" for past regrets.

7. I would rather _____ by train than by plane last weekend.

B. to travel
C. have travelled

Incorrect. Past simple (stopped) would express a hypothetical present situation, not a desire for behavior change.

Sentence Building Practice

Create your own sentences using the grammar patterns you've learned.

Practice 1

Create a sentence using "wish" about something you would like to be different in your job or studies.

Useful words: time, skills, location, colleagues, knowledge, experience

Example: I wish I had more time to learn new skills at work.

Practice 2

Write a sentence using "if only" to express a regret about something you did or didn't do in the past.

Useful words: studied, learned, traveled, saved, told, asked, applied

Example: If only I had learned Spanish when I was younger.

Practice 3

Create a sentence using "would rather" to express a preference between two activities.

Useful words: work, study, travel, stay, meet, cook, eat

Example: I would rather work from home than commute to the office every day.

Practice 4

Write a sentence using "would rather have" to express a preference about a past situation.

Useful words: gone, stayed, taken, spoken, asked, waited, chosen

Example: I would rather have taken the earlier train yesterday.
