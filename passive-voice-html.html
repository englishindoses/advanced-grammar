Passive Voice: Fundamentals

Introduction

Passive Voice changes how we present information in English. Instead of focusing on who does an action, it focuses on what receives the action. Passive voice is commonly used in business emails, academic papers, and professional communication.

Learning passive voice will help you:

  • Write more formal and objective business communications
  • Focus on actions and results instead of who performed them
  • Create more varied and sophisticated sentences

Explanation

Basic Definition

Passive voice focuses on the receiver of an action rather than the doer. We use it when who did the action is unknown, unimportant, or when we want to emphasize what happened rather than who did it.

Structure/Formation

Basic Formula: Subject + BE (in correct tense) + Past Participle (+ by + doer - optional)

Active to Passive Transformation:

ACTIVE: Subject + Verb + Object ‚Üì ‚Üì ‚Üì PASSIVE: Object + BE + Past Participle (+ by + Subject)

Example:

  • Active: The chef (subject) prepared (verb) the meal (object).
  • Passive: The meal (now subject) was prepared (BE + past participle) by the chef (by + doer).

Key Rules

  1. Only transitive verbs can be used in passive voice.

    Transitive verbs are verbs that take an object. Examples: build, make, write, send, clean

    ‚úì "The house was built last year." (build is transitive)
    ‚úó "The dog was slept in the garden." (sleep is intransitive)

  2. The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
  3. The main verb must always be in past participle form.

Usage Contexts

  • When the doer is unknown:
    The window was broken during the night.
  • When the doer is unimportant:
    The streets are cleaned every morning.
  • To emphasize the receiver:
    Our proposal was accepted by the committee.
  • In formal/academic/scientific writing:
    The experiment was conducted under controlled conditions.
  • To sound more objective:
    It has been decided that the project will continue.
  • To avoid mentioning responsibility:
    Mistakes were made in the calculation.

Examples in Context

Workplace Examples

  • "The report must be submitted by Friday." (Rather than "You must submit the report")
  • "The new policy has been implemented by management." (Focus on the policy)
  • "All emails should be checked before being sent to clients." (General rule, no specific doer)

Daily Life Examples

  • "My car is being repaired at the garage." (Focus on the car, not the mechanic)
  • "The package will be delivered tomorrow." (The delivery person isn't important)
  • "English is spoken in many countries." (General fact)

Academic/Formal Examples

  • "The data were collected through surveys and interviews." (Scientific writing)
  • "It can be concluded that the hypothesis is valid." (Academic writing)
  • "The cultural implications have been examined in recent studies." (Research paper)

Dialogue Examples

At work:

Manager: "How's the Johnson project coming along?"

Employee: "The initial analysis has been completed, and the results are being reviewed by the team. The final report will be delivered by next week."

At a hotel:

Guest: "Is my room ready?"

Receptionist: "Yes, your room has been prepared. Your luggage will be brought up shortly."

Summary

Key Points

  • Passive voice shifts focus from the doer to the receiver of an action.
  • Form: Subject + BE verb (in correct tense) + past participle (+ by + agent).
  • Use passive when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or to sound more formal/objective.

Active vs. Passive Forms

Tense Active Structure Passive Structure
Present Simple They clean the office. The office is cleaned (by them).
Present Continuous They are cleaning the office. The office is being cleaned (by them).
Past Simple They cleaned the office. The office was cleaned (by them).
Past Continuous They were cleaning the office. The office was being cleaned (by them).
Present Perfect They have cleaned the office. The office has been cleaned (by them).
Past Perfect They had cleaned the office. The office had been cleaned (by them).
Future Simple They will clean the office. The office will be cleaned (by them).

Useful Tips

  1. Use passive voice less often in conversation - Too much passive can make speech sound overly formal. It's more common in writing than in casual speaking.
  2. Pay attention to the preposition "by" - Only use "by" when you need to specify who did the action. In many cases, passive voice is used precisely to avoid mentioning who did something.
  3. Notice passive voice in news and business communications - Look for examples in newspaper headlines, official announcements, and company emails.

üìù GRAMMAR TIP: When deciding between active and passive voice, ask yourself: "What is more important‚Äîwho did the action, or what happened?" Use passive when the action itself or its recipient deserves the focus.
